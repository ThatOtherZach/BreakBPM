## LINKEDIN — POSTS

**1 — The product story (mythos as a design tool)**

I shipped a product that's 25 years "late."

BreakBPM is a pool (billiards) scoring app I built — but it ships wearing a costume. It's presented as lost software from Saym Software Systems, a late-'90s shop whose pool tracker was shelved by the Y2K bug and "never released, until now."

That mythos isn't a gimmick I bolted on at the end. I designed the whole product around it. The interface looks like Windows 98. The manual reads like a boxed-software booklet. The landing page is the "back of the box."

Why make a 2026 web app look 25 years old? Because a clear story makes a thousand small decisions for you. Once the costume existed, I always knew what a button should look like, how the copy should sound, what to add, and what to cut.

The lesson I keep relearning: a strong concept is a decision-making engine, not decoration.

It's free to play in any browser. 🎱 breakbpm.com

---

**2 — A business-logic decision (Lucky Break / provably fair)**

The strangest feature I've ever shipped — and the one I'm most proud of.

BreakBPM (my pool app) has a $4.99 pass called Lucky Break. When you buy it, you're guaranteed at least a 30-day pass, with roughly a 1-in-5 chance it upgrades to Lifetime.

Here's the part I care about: the outcome isn't a black box. It's a seeded draw built from the last 30 days of the whole community's real pool shots. The odds are published before you buy, and the same inputs always produce the same result. The shot data only seeds the draw — it can't move the odds.

In plain terms: it's provably fair. A player can check, after the fact, that the game wasn't rigged against them.

I'm one person building a fun retro app, and I still chose to engineer this like it mattered — because trust is the actual product. If people can't trust you with the small money, they won't trust you with the big stuff.

Build the honest version. 🎱 breakbpm.com

---

**3 — The pricing decision (no subscriptions)**

I built a paid app with no subscriptions. On purpose.

The standard advice is that recurring revenue is the only way. But as a user, I'm tired of free trials that quietly bill me and "plans" I forget to cancel.

So BreakBPM does the opposite. You buy days of access — anywhere from 1 to 365 — and the per-day price drops the more days you add. Nothing auto-renews. Want three days for a tournament weekend? Buy three days.

Is it "optimal" for revenue? Probably not. But it's honest, and it matches how people actually use a pool app: in bursts, around game nights and trips.

Sometimes the right business model is just the one you'd want as a customer. 🎱 breakbpm.com

---

**4 — Credibility as a feature (verified-hall leaderboards)**

How do you give a pool hall its own leaderboard without people cheating from their couch?

In BreakBPM, every verified pool hall has a local leaderboard. To add a game to it, you have to finish a real 1-on-1 game, be standing in the venue (we confirm you're within ~300m), and you only get to tag each game once.

It's a small feature, but it took the most care — because the entire point is that the board means something. A leaderboard you can fake is just a list.

The principle I took from it: when a feature's whole value is its credibility, you spend your effort on the anti-cheat, not the animations. 🎱 breakbpm.com
